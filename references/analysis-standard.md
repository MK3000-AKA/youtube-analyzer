# Evidence-based analysis standard

## 1. Classify the video

Label the content before judging it:

- independent review
- sponsored review
- affiliate showcase
- first look or launch coverage
- tutorial
- cinematic demonstration
- long-term owner report

Disclosure affects confidence, but sponsorship alone does not make footage false.

## 2. Separate evidence layers

Use four distinct layers:

1. **Public facts**: title, date, statistics, description, chapters and declared sponsorship.
2. **Creator claims**: statements in the transcript.
3. **Visible demonstrations**: capabilities directly shown in the footage.
4. **Independent evidence**: user comments, owner reports, primary documentation and reputable external tests.

Do not promote a claim from one layer into another without support.

## 3. Find the missing tests

For products, check whether the video covers:

- long-duration reliability and overheating
- battery life under realistic workloads
- focus failures and recovery
- waterproofing, dust, drops and vibration
- default output versus edited or graded output
- accessories, crew and hidden workflow costs
- formal firmware versus prerelease firmware
- direct same-generation competitor comparison

A visually strong showcase usually proves performance ceiling, not reliability.

## 4. Analyze comments

Start with the highest-liked comments, then search the full sample for:

- actual owners
- specific failures
- purchase or preorder intent
- sponsorship concerns
- competitor comparisons
- questions the video left unanswered

Generic praise is weak evidence. A detailed owner report is high-value but still a single case until corroborated.

Comment sentiment percentages are approximate classifications of the collected sample, not the entire audience.

## 5. Verify current claims

Browse current sources when claims concern prices, availability, lawsuits, product specifications, firmware, corporate announcements or other rapidly changing facts.

Prefer:

1. manufacturer documentation and filings
2. primary announcements
3. independent hands-on reviews

Clearly distinguish verification from inference.

## 6. Write the conclusion

For product analysis, answer:

- What is genuinely new or useful?
- What does this video prove?
- What does it fail to prove?
- What do comments confirm or contradict?
- Who benefits from the product?
- Who should wait?
- What should be tested next?

Be direct. Do not convert enthusiasm into a purchase recommendation without evidence.
